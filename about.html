Indoor Plant Types

Air Purifying Plants

Also known as "air-cleaning plants," are indoor plants that are believed to improve indoor air quality by removing harmful pollutants and chemicals from the air. These pollutants can include volatile organic compounds (VOCs) like formaldehyde and benzene, as well as toxins like carbon monoxide and nitrogen dioxide.
While the effectiveness of air purifying plants in improving indoor air quality is still a subject of debate, some studies have suggested that certain types of plants can help to remove these harmful chemicals from the air, at least to some extent. Some of the most commonly cited air purifying plants include spider plants, peace lilies, snake plants, and English ivy.
In addition to their potential air-cleaning properties, air purifying plants can also provide other benefits to indoor environments, such as adding moisture to dry air and enhancing the overall aesthetics of a room.

Low Light Plants

Are plants that can survive and thrive in environments with limited exposure to natural or artificial light. Typically, these plants have adapted to lower light levels in their native habitats, such as forests floors or shaded areas, and as such they do not require as much sunlight as other plants to photosynthesize and grow.

Low light indoor plants are ideal for spaces that receive little natural light, such as basements, bathrooms, or offices with few windows. However, it's important to note that while these plants can survive in low light conditions, they still need some light to grow and maintain their health. Therefore, it's important to choose the right type of low light plant for your specific indoor environment and to provide them with adequate care, such as proper watering and soil conditions.

Pet-Friendly Plants

Pet-friendly plants are indoor or outdoor plants that are considered safe for pets to be around, meaning that they are not toxic or harmful to cats, dogs, or other animals if they happen to ingest them.

It's important to note that even pet-friendly plants can cause some gastrointestinal discomfort or mild symptoms if ingested in large quantities, so it's still best to supervise your pets around plants and discourage them from eating them. Additionally, while a plant may be safe for one type of pet, it may not be safe for another, so it's always a good idea to check with your veterinarian or a reputable plant resource before introducing a new plant to your home or garden.
